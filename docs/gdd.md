# Season of Warlords — GDD

## 1. Game Identity
**Season of Warlords** is a medieval fantasy strategy RPG centered on a fragile faction based in **Cheongram Castle**. The player does not begin as a conquering emperor; they begin as a surviving remnant that must choose fights carefully, protect people, and rebuild legitimacy through action.

The core emotional arc is built around **companion recruitment and castle growth**. New allies are not disposable units; they are narrative milestones, tactical options, and symbols that the player is no longer alone. Every meaningful recruit should change both battle options and castle atmosphere.

The WorldMap exists primarily as an **expedition and event selection layer**, not as a conquest-only board. A region can host a frontline battle, a rescue, a rumor-driven investigation, a defensive stand, or a high-stakes choice event. Control of territory matters, but the immediate question is often: *what situation is happening there now?*

Battles remain a major pillar, but they are not the only content type. Some progress beats should resolve through dialogue choices, investigations, escort outcomes, or social consequences. This ensures pacing variety and reinforces the game identity as a strategy RPG with narrative momentum.

In short, the game identity is: **small-faction survival -> trusted companions -> living castle -> expanding agency in a changing region**.

## 2. Core Fantasy
The player should feel that they are building strength from almost nothing. Early on, each expedition is risky, each rumor is uncertain, and each resource gain matters.

As rumors spread, the player gathers unusual allies who would not join a strong empire but may join a principled, growing faction. Companions are discovered through local crises: border conflicts, forest incidents, ancient mysteries, and civilian emergencies.

The castle must feel like home and responsibility at the same time. The player returns from each mission to see practical growth (resources, upgrades, unlocks) and social growth (new people, new conversations, new pressure).

Over time, the fantasy shifts from mere survival to regional influence: the player is still not the largest power, but becomes a credible one through decisive responses to events.

## 3. Core Loop
**Castle Hub -> Rumor -> Expedition -> Encounter -> Reward/Recruit -> Return -> Growth**

- **Castle Hub**: Review status, talk to companions, check facilities, and decide priorities.
- **Rumor**: Receive or activate a lead that frames a goal and destination.
- **Expedition**: Move to the target region via WorldMap and commit resources/time.
- **Encounter**: Resolve the region situation through battle, choice, rescue, investigation, defense, or mixed flow.
- **Reward/Recruit**: Gain resources, reputation, class unlocks, and/or a new companion.
- **Return**: Come back to Cheongram to process consequences.
- **Growth**: Upgrade facilities, unlock options, trigger new rumors, and open further region chains.

## 4. Main Pillars
1. **Castle as home**  
   Cheongram is the emotional and strategic anchor where progress is made visible.

2. **Companions as progression**  
   Recruitment is a core reward type, not side content; companions drive narrative and tactics.

3. **Regions as stories, not just land**  
   Regions represent evolving situations, not static ownership targets.

4. **Battles as dramatic events**  
   Battles must matter narratively and mechanically, but are part of a larger event ecosystem.

5. **Small faction growth**  
   Power rises step-by-step through survival decisions, not through immediate map domination.

## 5. What This Game Is Not
- Not a pure grand strategy game.
- Not a pure tactical RPG mission ladder.
- Not a conquest-only map painting game.
- Not a random battle grinder.
- Not a huge open-world sandbox.

## 6. Early Game Promise
Within the first 30-60 minutes, the player should understand the full thesis of the game.

Leon starts at Cheongram Castle as the default leader and stable anchor. Through a military/frontier path, the player reaches the Northern Watch Fort and recruits Garon. This demonstrates that rumors can lead to battle-driven companion recruitment.

After Garon, a forest/gate rumor leads to the Frost Forest Gate and Elin recruitment, reinforcing continuity and party growth. The player now sees that castle return, rumor progression, and companion visibility are tightly connected.

Next, Mira is introduced through an **Ancient Ruins investigation**. Ancient Ruins is already player-owned, but still contains unresolved internal danger. This teaches a key rule: regions are not only conquest nodes; they can also be investigation, rescue, defense, or choice content.

By the end of this sequence, the player should believe that each new rumor can reshape both battlefield options and life at the castle.

## 7. Long-term Expansion Direction
- Expand companion roster with distinct tactical identities and relationship arcs.
- Add class unlock branches tied to events, facilities, and companion milestones.
- Grow castle facilities into meaningful strategic systems.
- Introduce faction pressure (raids, political threats, supply strain) without shifting into full grand strategy.
- Build region chains where one event unlocks or transforms another.
- Add companion personal quests that alter trust, roles, and endings.
- Implement recurring castle defense events to reinforce home protection stakes.
